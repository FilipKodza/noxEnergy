Standard Sources Of Electricity

Electricity is essential for modern life, yet almost one billion people live without access to it. Challenges such as climate change, pollution and environmental destruction require that we change the way we generate electricity.


Over the past century, the main energy sources used for generating electricity have been fossil fuels, hydroelectricity and, since the 1950s, nuclear energy. Despite the strong growth of renewables over the last few decades, fossil-based fuels remain dominant worldwide. Their use for electricity generation continues to increase in both absolute and relative terms: in 2017, fossil fuels generated 64.5% of worldwide electricity, compared with 61.9% in 1990.


Access to reliable electricity is vital for human wellbeing. Currently one in seven people in the world has no access to electricity. As such, electricity demand will continue to rise. At the same time, greenhouse gas emissions must decrease drastically if we are to mitigate climate change, and we must switch to cleaner sources of energy to reduce air pollution. This will likely require large increases of all low-carbon energy sources, of which nuclear is an important part.


In order to achieve a sustainable world, all sectors of the economy will need to be decarbonized, including transport, heat and industry. Electricity provides the means to utilize low-carbon energy sources, and so widespread electrification is seen as a key tool for decarbonising sectors traditionally powered by fossil fuels. As the end uses for electricity grow, and as the benefits of electricity are extended to all people, demand will grow significantly.

Coal


Coal has been a critical energy source and a mainstay of global energy production for centuries. However, it is also the most polluting fossil fuel, both in terms of the CO2 it produces per unit of energy and the amount of local air pollution it generates. Moving away from coal is therefore a key priority for both climate change mitigation and public health.


Despite the rapid growth of renewable energy, coal remains the single largest source of electricity generation worldwide, accounting for roughly 35% of global power generation, a share that has declined only gradually from around 40% a decade earlier. Global coal consumption reached an all-time high of nearly 8.8 billion tonnes, with the power sector responsible for about two-thirds of total demand.


The picture varies enormously between countries. China alone accounts for around a third of global coal consumption and continues to set new records in both production and use, even as it expands its nuclear, solar, and wind capacity. India has also seen strong growth in coal demand. By contrast, coal use continues to fall in the European Union and the United States, and some countries have phased it out almost entirely: the United Kingdom, once heavily reliant on coal, closed its last coal-fired power plant in 2024.


This divergence reflects a broader pattern: while many advanced economies are shifting away from coal as renewable energy becomes more cost-competitive, demand in developing economies, particularly in Asia, continues to rise as electricity needs grow faster than clean energy capacity can be added.

Oil


Oil remains the world's largest single energy source, accounting for roughly 30% of global primary energy consumption, a share that has fallen steadily from a peak of around 46% several decades ago. Unlike coal and gas, oil plays only a minor role in electricity generation; its dominance lies almost entirely in the transport sector, where it remains the primary fuel for road vehicles, aviation, and shipping.


Global oil demand has continued to grow, though at a much slower pace than in previous decades. Much of this recent growth has come not from transport but from petrochemical feedstocks, used to manufacture plastics, resins, and other oil-based products, as well as aviation fuel. Demand growth in road transport, traditionally oil's largest use, has slowed sharply in recent years due to the rising adoption of electric vehicles and the growing use of alternative fuels such as liquefied natural gas in trucking.


The United States and China are the world's two largest oil consumers by a wide margin. While China was a major driver of global oil demand growth in the years following the COVID-19 pandemic, its consumption growth has since slowed considerably, partly due to the rapid rollout of electric vehicles, expansion of high-speed rail, and a slowdown in its broader economy.


As electric vehicle adoption continues to expand worldwide, oil's role in road transport is expected to gradually diminish. However, its continued importance in aviation, shipping, and petrochemical production means oil is likely to remain a significant part of the global energy mix for years to come.

Gas


Natural gas lagged behind coal and oil as an energy source for decades, but its use has grown rapidly in recent years, often replacing coal in the electricity mix. Gas is a major source of electricity generation and a key fuel for heating.


Gas is now the second-largest source of electricity production globally, accounting for roughly 22-23% of the world's electricity generation, behind only coal. Global gas-fired electricity generation reached a record high of close to 6,800 terawatt-hours, although its overall market share has declined slightly in recent years as electricity demand has grown faster than gas supply.


The United States is by far the world's largest producer of electricity from gas, generating more than three times as much as Russia, the second-largest producer. Gas use is also expanding quickly in parts of Asia, Latin America, and the Middle East, where it is helping countries diversify away from coal and oil. In Europe, by contrast, gas generation has been declining for several years as wind and solar capacity continues to expand.


From a climate perspective, the shift from coal to gas is generally a positive one, since gas typically emits significantly less CO2 per unit of energy produced. However, gas is still a fossil fuel, and the long-term goal remains a shift away from it toward low-carbon sources such as renewables and nuclear power.

Decarbonization

No energy source comes without some negative impact, but the differences between sources are enormous. Energy production can affect human health and the environment in three main ways: air pollution, accidents across the supply chain, and greenhouse gas emissions. Fossil fuels rank worst on all three counts, while nuclear and renewable sources rank consistently low, making the case for decarbonization both a climate issue and a public health issue.


There is no trade-off between the safest energy sources in the short term and the least damaging in the long term, they are the same sources. Coal is both the deadliest and the dirtiest fuel, emitting over a hundred times more greenhouse gases per unit of electricity than nuclear power. Oil and gas are also significantly worse than nuclear and renewables, though less so than coal.


Fossil fuels still account for around 60% of the global electricity mix. Transitioning away from them toward nuclear and renewable energy would reduce both deaths from accidents and air pollution, as well as greenhouse gas emissions, delivering health benefits today alongside climate benefits for future generations.


Achieving a sustainable world will require decarbonizing every sector of the economy, including transport, heating, and industry, not just electricity generation. Electrification is widely seen as a key tool for this transition, since it allows sectors traditionally powered by fossil fuels to be supplied instead by low-carbon electricity. As demand for electricity grows and access is extended to more people worldwide, a diverse mix of low-carbon sources, including nuclear, hydro, solar, and wind, will be needed to meet that demand sustainably.
